Fish oil for senior dogs: the joint supplement with real evidence behind it

Unlike a lot of the supplement aisle, omega-3s (EPA and DHA) actually have studies behind them for canine joints. Here's the honest version — what helps, what's hype, and the questions for your vet.

If you’ve read my honest take on glucosamine, you know I’m wary of joint supplements that promise more than the science delivers. Fish oil is the interesting exception — it’s one of the few where the research is genuinely more encouraging. That doesn’t make it a miracle, but it does make it worth understanding properly.

What the evidence actually says

Here’s the part that sets fish oil apart. According to Canine Arthritis Resources and Education (CARE), dogs need the long-chain omega-3s EPA and DHA to help dampen the inflammation tied to arthritis, and the most potent source is marine fish oil. A published systematic review found sufficient evidence to consider EPA/DHA as an adjunctive (add-on) therapy for dogs with osteoarthritis — and veterinary studies of diets high in omega-3s have shown improved weight-bearing and, in some dogs, a reduced dose of anti-inflammatory medication.

Two honest caveats, straight from CARE: most of that strong evidence comes from therapeutic diets with high, controlled omega-3 levels — the evidence for ordinary fish oil supplements is more limited, and there’s essentially none for feeding whole fish casually. And plant-based omega-3s like flaxseed don’t count here, because dogs can’t convert them into EPA and DHA well enough to matter for joints.

So the fair takeaway is: omega-3s have better evidence than most joint supplements, they support comfort as an add-on, and they work best as part of a plan your vet is guiding — not as a stand-alone fix.

Dosing and timing, in plain terms

A few practical points from CARE, all of which are worth confirming with your vet for your specific dog:

  • It’s EPA and DHA that count. Look at the combined EPA+DHA on the label, not the headline “total omega-3” number.
  • A common target is around 100 mg/kg of EPA+DHA per day (CARE notes a reported range of 50–220 mg/kg). Because products differ so much in concentration, the number of capsules or millilitres varies a lot — which is exactly why the dose is a vet conversation, not a guess.
  • Be patient. It takes about two months for omega-3s to build up in cell membranes, so don’t judge it before then.

What to check before you buy

Supplements aren’t tightly regulated, and quality is genuinely uneven. CARE’s cautions are worth repeating:

  • Look for third-party testing. A reputable brand can provide a certificate of analysis showing what’s actually in the bottle. CARE notes independent testing (ConsumerLab) has found some pet products contained only a fraction of their labeled omega-3s.
  • Mind freshness. Fish oils oxidize easily — keep them out of light, and refrigerate liquids after opening.
  • Count the calories. Fish oil is fat, so factor it in if your dog is also on a weight plan.
  • Flag other medications. Omega-3s can affect blood clotting, so tell your vet what else your dog takes.

Where this fits

If your senior dog is stiff or sore, fish oil is one reasonable, better-evidenced piece to discuss — but it’s a piece, not the plan. The things with the biggest, most reliable payoff are still the unglamorous ones: keeping your dog at a lean weight, making home easier on their joints, and getting a real veterinary read on what’s going on. A good supplement supports that work; it doesn’t replace it.

Not veterinary advice. Omega-3s are a comfort/support option to discuss with your vet — they don’t treat or cure arthritis or any medical condition, and dosing should be confirmed for your dog. The evidence here is stronger than for most joint supplements — which is notable, because it's often weaker than the labels claim. A systematic review concluded there's sufficient evidence to consider long-chain omega-3s (EPA and DHA) as an add-on for dogs with osteoarthritis, and veterinary studies of high-omega-3 diets have shown improved weight-bearing and, in some cases, a reduced need for anti-inflammatory medication.

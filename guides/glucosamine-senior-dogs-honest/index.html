Glucosamine for senior dogs: the real answer, and what giving it to my dog was actually like

The supplement aisle promises a lot. The science is murkier than that. Here's what the evidence actually says about glucosamine, and what it's really like to get a stubborn senior dog to take it.

If you’ve stood in the pet-store aisle staring at a wall of “hip and joint” chews for your aging dog, wondering whether any of them actually do anything — this is the straight answer I wish someone had given me. I’m trying glucosamine chews with my own thirteen-year-old, Chief (a 38-pound Mountain Cur and Pekingese mix who’s slowing down), so this is part what the research actually says and part what it’s really been like to give them to him.

I’ll warn you up front: this page won’t tell you glucosamine will fix your dog’s joints. I can’t — and as you’ll see, the science can’t either.

The real answer: what the evidence actually says

Here’s the thing the cheerful labels gloss over. Glucosamine is one of the most recommended joint supplements for dogs, and it’s been used for decades — but whether it actually works is genuinely contested, and recent research leans skeptical.

Some sources are cautiously positive: older reviews found certain glucosamine preparations gave a “moderate” level of comfort, and plenty of veterinarians have reported modest improvements in some dogs. But several recent, larger reviews have cast real doubt. A 2017 review of seventeen clinical trials concluded the clinical benefit remains questionable. A 2022 meta-analysis went further, reporting strong evidence of non-effect and concluding these supplements shouldn’t be prescribed for arthritis in dogs. There is no scientific consensus, and — this part matters — there is no supplement that cures arthritis.

So the takeaway isn’t “glucosamine works” or “glucosamine is useless.” It’s: the evidence is mixed and trending doubtful, the supplement is very safe, and it’s cheap — which is why a lot of owners (and some vets) still try it, while keeping expectations low. That’s a very different message than the bottle gives you, and it’s the truthful one.

Is it safe? (This part is clearer)

The safety picture is more settled than the effectiveness one. Glucosamine is considered one of the safer supplements for dogs — it’s regulated more like a food than a drug, and serious side effects are rare. At high doses it can occasionally cause increased thirst or a mild upset stomach. The one real caution: glucosamine is often made from shellfish, so avoid it if your dog has a shellfish allergy. And because many “joint” chews combine several ingredients, it’s always worth a quick check with your vet before starting — especially if your dog takes other medications.

For what it’s worth, Chief has had no digestive upset that I’ve noticed on them. That’s a real observation about tolerance — not a claim that they’re helping his joints, which I genuinely can’t know.

What it’s actually been like (my real experience)

Here’s the stuff no clinical review will tell you: getting a stubborn senior dog to eat the things.

The chews I’m trying are StrellaLab Max Strength Glucosamine Chews (their senior hip-and-joint soft chew). A few plain observations from daily life with them:

  • Chief is not a fan of the taste. Whatever else these are, they weren’t built to be irresistible. Chief — who is no dummy — will happily take a treat and then, somehow, leave the half-chewed supplement behind on the rug.
  • You have to be clever about dosing. What works for me is hiding the chew inside a treat he actually wants, or tucking it into something soft. Hand it to him plain and you’ll be finding it later, abandoned in a corner.
  • No stomach trouble. As above — he’s tolerated them fine, no upset that I’ve seen.
  • The dosing is weight-based. The label suggests two chews for dogs roughly 11–39 pounds, which covers Chief. Always follow the product’s weight chart, and confirm with your vet.

And my own verdict on them? Genuinely still out. I’m only partway into trying these, and I’m not ready to call them either a win or a waste — not yet. So far I can report the practical stuff (taste, tolerance, the dosing battle), but not a conclusion. If that changes as I keep going, I’ll say so.

None of that tells you whether they help — only what owning them is like. But “will my dog even eat these, and will they upset his stomach” is a real question people have, and it’s one I can answer truthfully.

So should you bother?

That’s genuinely your call to make with your vet, not mine to make for you. Here’s the framing I’d offer: glucosamine is safe and inexpensive, so trying it isn’t risky — but the published evidence that it helps is weak, so go in with low expectations and don’t let it stand in for an actual veterinary plan. (To be clear, that “weak evidence” is what the studies say about glucosamine in general — not my personal verdict on the specific chews I’m trying, which is still undecided.) If your dog is stiff or sore, the most useful thing isn’t picking the “best” chew off a shelf — it’s a conversation with your vet about the whole picture, which may include options with better evidence behind them, weight management, and comfort changes at home.

On that last point — the comfort-at-home part is something you can act on directly. If your dog is struggling to get around, I’ve written about ramps and stairs, supportive beds, and making your home easier for an aging dog. And if you’re still trying to sort normal aging from something worth a vet visit, start with the everyday signs of a dog slowing down.

Whatever you decide about a supplement, the one thing I’d hold onto is this: a chew from a bottle is not a substitute for your vet’s eyes on your dog. Use it as one small, optional piece — not the plan.
